# The Skeletal System

---

## Functions of the Skeletal System

1. Provides shape and form
2. Supports and protects internal organs
3. Creates structure for movement (muscle attachment sites)
4. Produces blood (in marrow)
5. Stores minerals
6. Determines posture

---

## Two Divisions of the Skeleton

| Division | Bone count | Components |
|---|---|---|
| **Axial** | ~80 bones | Skull, ribcage, vertebral column |
| **Appendicular** | ~126 bones | Arms, legs, pelvic girdle |

**Total:** ~206 bones, ~177 involved in voluntary movement, forming 300+ joints.

---

## Five Types of Bones

| Type | Characteristics | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Long** | Cylindrical shaft with widened ends | Femur |
| **Short** | Similar length and width, cube-like | Carpals (wrist) |
| **Flat** | Thin surfaces, protect organs | Sternum |
| **Irregular** | Unique shape and function | Vertebrae, sacrum |
| **Sesamoid** | Embedded in tendon over joint | Patella |

---

## Bone Surface Markings

**Depressions** (flattened/indented):
- **Fossa** — a common depression
- **Sulcus** — a groove allowing soft tissue passage

**Processes** (projections for attachments):
- **Condyles** — at bottom of femur / top of tibia (form knee joint)
- **Epicondyles** — on humerus (form elbow joint)
- **Tubercles** — on humerus (greater/lesser, attach shoulder muscles)
- **Trochanters** — on femur (attach hip muscles)
- **Spinous processes** — on vertebrae

---

## Bone Remodeling

Constant renewal through two processes:

> [!note] Memory Trick
> **osteoBLASTs Build / osteoCLASTs Crush**

- **Osteoclasts** — break DOWN old bone (resorption) — "Clasts Crush"
- **Osteoblasts** — BUILD new bone (formation) — "Blasts Build"

### Bone Mass Timeline

| Age range | What happens |
|---|---|
| Childhood–teens | Formation > removal (growing) |
| ~20–30 | Peak bone mass reached |
| 30–50 | Relatively stable |
| 50+ | Decline begins → osteoporosis risk |

### Exercise & Bone Health

**Weight-bearing = best for bones:**
- Resistance training
- Walking, jogging
- Body-weight squats, push-ups
- Climbing stairs
- Dancing

**Not optimal for bones (still good for muscles/cardio):**
- Swimming
- Cycling

> [!note] Pilates Reference
> Reformer work IS weight-bearing (resistance against springs). Mat Pilates = weight-bearing through gravity. Particularly important for female clients 40+ as a safer alternative to high-impact exercise that still delivers bone stimulus. See [[../Pilates Applications|Pilates Applications]].

**Why it matters:** Exercise, posture, activity level, and nutrition all affect bone growth, maturation, and functionality.

## The Vertebral Column

![[../assets/spine.jpg]]

```
    C1-C7  Cervical (neck) — 7 vertebrae, support head
      ↓
    T1-T12 Thoracic (mid-back) — 12 vertebrae, anchor ribs
      ↓
    L1-L5  Lumbar (lower back) — 5 vertebrae, largest, bear most weight
      ↓
    Sacrum — 5 fused vertebrae, triangular
      ↓
    Coccyx — 3-5 fused bones, tailbone
```

**Key structures:**
- **Intervertebral discs** — fibrous cartilage, shock absorbers, allow spine movement
- **Neutral spine** — optimal S-curve position where vertebrae are under least load

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Neutral Spine
> Neutral spine = the natural S-curve of the vertebral column at rest. In Pilates:
> - **Neutral pelvis** preserves this curve (ASIS and pubic symphysis in the same horizontal plane when supine)
> - **Imprinting** slightly flattens the lumbar curve against the mat — used for beginners or hypermobile clients
> - Excessive flexion of the spine stresses the intervertebral discs (see [[../Biomechanics & Kinesiology/Biomechanics & Kinesiology#Applied Biomechanics — Squat Analysis|Squat Analysis]])

## Joints — Classification

**Two main types:**

| Type | Movement | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-synovial** | Little to no movement | Skull sutures |
| **Synovial** | Free movement (~80% of joints) | Hip, knee, shoulder |

---

## Six Types of Synovial Joints

| Joint | Movement | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Ball & socket** | All three directions (most mobile) | Hip, shoulder |
| **Hinge** | One direction like a door | Elbow, knee, toes |
| **Pivot** | Rotation around axis | Atlantoaxial (base of skull), proximal radioulnar |
| **Gliding/plane** | Non-axial, back/forth or side/side (simplest) | Carpals of wrist, vertebrae, ankle |
| **Condyloid** | Predominantly one direction, minimal others | Fingers, jaw |
| **Saddle** | Back-forth + side-side, limited rotation | Carpometacarpal of thumb |

---

## Arthrokinematics vs Osteokinematics

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Osteokinematic** | Movements we can SEE (e.g. elbow bending) |
| **Arthrokinematic** | Movements INSIDE the joint (roll, slide, spin) |

**Three arthrokinematic movement types:**
- **Roll** — like a tyre on road (femur on tibia in squat)
- **Slide** — like a skid (tibia on femur in knee extension)
- **Spin** — like twisting a jar lid (forearm pronation/supination)

---

## Synovial Joint Components

- **Joint capsule** — loosely holds bones together
- **Ligaments** — static/dynamic stability
- **Cartilage** — pads ends of articulating bones
- **Synovial membrane** — secretes synovial fluid
- **Synovial fluid** — lubricates joint surfaces (like engine oil), nourishes cartilage

---

## Ligaments

> [!note] Definition
> **Ligaments** connect BONE to BONE. Primary protein: **collagen** (tensile strength). Secondary protein: **elastin** (flexibility/elastic recoil).

- **Poor vascularity** (blood supply) → slow to heal
- Provide joint stability + sensory input to nervous system ([[../Nervous System/Nervous System#Proprioception|proprioception]])

**Example:** ACL has very little elastin, mostly collagen — great for resisting strong forces, excellent knee stabiliser.

> [!note] Memory Trick
> **Ligament = bone to bone / Tendon = muscle to bone** (see [[../Muscular System/Muscular System#Tendons vs Ligaments|Tendons vs Ligaments]])
