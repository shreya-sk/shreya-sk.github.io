# The Nervous System

---

## Three Primary Functions

| Function | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Sensory** | Detects changes internally + externally |
| **Integrative** | Analyses + interprets sensory info, makes decisions |
| **Motor** | Body's response via neuromuscular system |

---

## Two Main Divisions

![[../assets/cns.jpg]]

```
NERVOUS SYSTEM
│
├── CENTRAL NS (CNS) — interprets information
│   ├── Brain
│   └── Spinal cord
│
└── PERIPHERAL NS (PNS) — connects CNS to body
    ├── 12 cranial nerves
    ├── 31 pairs of spinal nerves
    └── Sensory receptors
```

---

## Neuron Anatomy

- **Cell body (soma)** — contains nucleus + organelles, controls cell functions
- **Axon** — cylindrical projection, sends signals OUT to other neurons/muscles/organs
- **Dendrites** — gather info from other structures, transmit INTO neuron

**Key electrolytes for nerve function:** Sodium, potassium, magnesium, water — must be adequate for proper nerve impulse transmission.

---

## Pathways

| Pathway | Direction | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Afferent** (sensory) | PNS → CNS (up to brain) | Sensory info |
| **Efferent** (motor) | CNS → PNS (down to body) | Motor commands |
| **Interneurons** | Within CNS only | Between afferent/efferent |

> [!note] Memory Tricks
> - **Afferent = Arrives** (at brain)
> - **Efferent = Exits** (from brain)

## PNS Further Subdivisions

![[../assets/pns.jpg]]

```
Peripheral Nervous System
│
├── SOMATIC NS → voluntary movement (skeletal muscles)
│
└── AUTONOMIC NS → involuntary (organs, glands)
    │
    ├── SYMPATHETIC → "Fight or Flight"
    │   ↑ HR, ↑ breathing, muscles ready, adrenaline released
    │   (active during exercise)
    │
    └── PARASYMPATHETIC → "Rest and Digest"
        ↓ HR, ↓ breathing, recovery, digestion
```

**Fitness relevance:**
- Chronic stress → overactive sympathetic → can't rest/recover
- Healthy clients need balance between the two
- Breathing exercises and slow cardio shift toward parasympathetic

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Breath and the Autonomic NS
> Lateral thoracic breathing (the Pilates breath) activates the parasympathetic NS. This is why Pilates can reduce anxiety and stress — it's not just psychological, it's a neurological shift via controlled respiration. See [[../Cardiorespiratory System/Cardiorespiratory System#Breathing (Ventilation)|Cardiorespiratory System]] for breathing mechanics.

## Mechanoreceptors — The Three Critical Types

| Receptor | Location | Responds to | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Muscle spindles** | Within muscle, parallel to fibres | Change in muscle LENGTH + rate of change | Stretch reflex — prevents overstretching (response in 1-2 ms) |
| **Golgi Tendon Organs (GTO)** | At muscle-tendon insertion | Change in muscle TENSION + rate of change | Triggers muscle relaxation to prevent injury |
| **Joint receptors** | In/around joint capsule | Pressure, acceleration, deceleration | Signals extreme joint positions |

**Both spindles and GTOs are critical for flexibility training.**

> [!note] Pilates Application
> **Held stretches activate GTOs → muscle relaxes deeper.** This is the physiological reason slow, held stretches work better than bouncing. Classical Pilates stretch cues are grounded in this mechanism.
>
> **Muscle spindles** trigger the **stretch reflex** — rapid bouncing triggers spindles → protective contraction. Slow movement bypasses spindles → allows deeper range.

## Proprioception

> [!note] Definition
> The body's natural ability to sense its general orientation and the relative position of all its parts — without looking.

**Training proprioception improves:**
- Balance
- Coordination
- Posture
- Injury prevention
- Adapting to surroundings without conscious thought

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Proprioceptive Training
> Single-leg work, balance challenges, and unstable surfaces (Pilates arc, half-foam roller, proprioceptive discs) train the nervous system, not just muscles. This is why Pilates improves balance in older adults — it trains proprioception. Mat work on softer surfaces deepens the challenge further.

## Motor Skill Learning — Three Stages

Critical for teaching Pilates clients new exercises:

| Stage | What's happening | How to teach |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Cognitive** | Client is new to skill, inconsistent performance | Simple instructions, break skill into small steps |
| **2. Associative** | Client is getting it, understanding the skill | Refine through practice and regular feedback |
| **3. Autonomous** | Skill mastered, performed automatically | Challenge with variations, add complexity |

**Background science:** Motor skill learning relates to brain development through:
- **Neuroplasticity** — brain's ability to change
- **Neurocircuitry** — neural pathway formation

> [!note] Pilates Cueing Application
> - Cognitive stage clients: one cue at a time, simple language
> - Over-cueing = cognitive overload → disrupts learning
> - Tactile cues can bypass cognitive processing when verbal cues fail (e.g., placing hand on client's ribcage to cue breath)
> - Autonomous clients need novelty and complexity to keep the nervous system adapting

---

## Nervous System Development

- Adolescent brain develops from age 10 to 25
- Influenced by heredity, environment, medications
- CNS continues to acquire new skills throughout life ("old dogs can learn new tricks")
- Physical and cognitive decline with aging — **physical fitness is one of the best counters**

---

## Early Strength Gains Are Neural

> [!note] Key Insight
> Early strength gains from exercise = **nervous system adaptations**, not muscle size. The body learns to recruit the right muscle at the right time with better motor unit synchronisation. Muscle hypertrophy comes later.

This is especially relevant for new Pilates clients who feel "stronger" in weeks 1–4 — they are — but it's neural, not structural.

---

## Applied Example — Nervous System in Training

Using nervous system knowledge in programming:
1. **Stability & coordination** — single-leg stands, stability ball → challenges [[#Proprioception|proprioception]]
2. **Strength through neural activation** — plyometrics → CNS learns to fire many motor units simultaneously
3. **Progressive loading** — slow-twitch training first, then fast-twitch
4. **Injury prevention** — form first, then complexity
5. **Feedback loops** — direction changes and agility drills improve CNS-PNS communication
