# The Kinetic Chain

---

## What Is the Kinetic Chain?

The **kinetic chain** is the human movement system — three interdependent systems working together:

| System | Role |
|---|---|
| **[[../Nervous System/Nervous System\|Nervous System]]** | Control centre — tells muscles when/how to contract |
| **[[../Skeletal System/Skeletal System\|Skeletal System]]** | Structural framework — bones as rigid levers |
| **[[../Muscular System/Muscular System\|Muscular System]]** | Movers and stabilisers — produces tension and movement |

> [!note] Core Principle
> Dysfunction in ONE system affects the OTHER TWO. Efficient movement requires all three working in harmony.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              THE KINETIC CHAIN                  │
│                                                 │
│   NERVOUS ──► MUSCULAR ──► SKELETAL             │
│  (controls)   (moves)      (framework)          │
│       ▲                       │                 │
│       └───────  feedback ─────┘                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

## Joint Function & Kinetic Chain

**Key concept:** All joints are linked. Movement of one directly affects others.

_Demonstration:_ Roll feet inward/outward → knees and hips move too. This is why if one joint isn't working correctly, it affects all joints it works with.

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Powerhouse
> In Pilates, the kinetic chain is the basis of the **powerhouse** concept: the deep stabilisers of the lumbo-pelvic-hip complex (TVA, multifidus, pelvic floor, diaphragm) must be activated before limb movement to protect the spine. This is feed-forward activation in practice. See [[../Muscular System/Muscular System#Feed Forward Activation|feed-forward activation]] and [[../Pilates Applications|Pilates Applications]].

## Muscle Imbalances & Length-Tension Relationship

### Ideal Muscle Balance

- Both sides of joint have optimal length
- **Ideal length-tension relationship** = optimal tension for optimal force

### Imbalance Pattern

```
Short/overactive muscle ← → Long/underactive muscle
(tight, too much tone)    (lengthened, weak, inhibited)
```

**Short/overactive** = pulls bone toward it (tension bias)
**Long/underactive** = allows the other muscle to pull bone out of position

**Result:** Joint in suboptimal position → increased stress → risk of injury.

## Regional Interdependence — The Domino Effect

Joints depend on other joints. Dysfunction cascades.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│    KINETIC CHAIN DYSFUNCTION        │
│                                     │
│  Subtalar pronation                 │
│  (collapsed foot arch)              │
│           ▼                         │
│  Internal tibial rotation           │
│           ▼                         │
│  Internal femoral rotation          │
│           ▼                         │
│  Hip drop (same side)               │
│           ▼                         │
│  Shoulder compensation              │
│           ▼                         │
│  Upper body misalignment            │
│                                     │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

> [!note] Key Insight
> One imbalance anywhere creates a chain reaction throughout the body. This is why Pilates and fitness professionals assess **whole-body posture** — not just the problem area.

## Pilates Instructor Reference

> [!note] Postural Assessment in Pilates
> Before beginning client programming, assess:
> - **Foot position** — pronation/supination (see [[../Biomechanics & Kinesiology/Biomechanics & Kinesiology#Foot — Multiplanar Movement|foot multiplanar movement]])
> - **Knee tracking** — valgus (inward) or varus (outward) deviation
> - **Pelvic tilt** — anterior (hip flexor dominance) or posterior (glute inhibition)
> - **Neutral pelvis vs imprinted spine** — In Pilates, neutral pelvis preserves the natural lumbar curve. Imprinting (slight posterior tilt) is used for beginners or when stability requires reduced lumbar range. Neither is "wrong" — context determines which is appropriate.
> - **Spinal curves** — thoracic kyphosis, lumbar lordosis, cervical position
> - **Scapular position** — protracted, elevated, or winging (see [[../Biomechanics & Kinesiology/Biomechanics & Kinesiology#Scapular Movements|scapular movements]])
> - **Head position** — forward head posture increases cervical load

**Common imbalance patterns in Pilates clients:**

| Pattern | Short/Overactive | Long/Underactive |
|---|---|---|
| Anterior pelvic tilt | Hip flexors, lumbar extensors | Glutes, abdominals |
| Forward head posture | Pec minor, SCM, scalenes | Deep neck flexors, lower traps |
| Rounded shoulders | Pec major/minor | Rhomboids, middle/lower traps |
| Flat back | Hamstrings, abdominals | Hip flexors, lumbar extensors |
