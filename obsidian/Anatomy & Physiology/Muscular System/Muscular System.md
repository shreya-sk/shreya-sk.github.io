# The Muscular System

---

## Three Types of Muscle

| Type | Voluntary? | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Skeletal** | Voluntary | Attached to bones | Movement, posture |
| **Cardiac** | Involuntary | Heart | Pump blood |
| **Smooth** | Involuntary | Organs, digestive tract | Organ function |

---

## Functions of the Muscular System

1. Produce movement
2. Provide posture
3. Stabilise the body
4. Generate heat (homeostasis)
5. Support skeletal system

---

## Muscle Structure

Think of Russian nesting dolls — each layer wraps the one inside it:

```
Muscle (whole)
  └── Epimysium (deep fascia, wraps whole muscle)
      └── Fascicles (bundles of fibres)
          └── Perimysium (wraps each fascicle)
              └── Muscle fibres (cells)
                  └── Sarcolemma (plasma membrane of fibre)
                      └── Sarcoplasm (contains glycogen, fats, minerals, myoglobin, mitochondria)
                          └── Myofibrils
                              └── Sarcomeres ← contraction happens here
                                  └── Myofilaments: Actin (thin) + Myosin (thick)
```

> [!note] Key Terms
> - **Sarcomere** = functional unit of muscle contraction (where it actually happens)
> - **Z-line** = boundary between sarcomeres
> - **Actin** = thin filament | **Myosin** = thick filament

---

## Connective Tissues of Muscle

- Extend length of muscle → form **tendon** at ends
- Transmit forces from muscle to bone
- Allow muscles to work as functional groups

---

## Tendons vs Ligaments

> [!note] Easy Confusion Point
> 
> | | Tendons | Ligaments |
> |---|---|---|
> | Connect | **Muscle to bone** | **Bone to bone** |
> | Blood supply | Poor | Poor |
> | Healing | Slow | Slow |

See [[../Skeletal System/Skeletal System#Ligaments|Ligaments]] for full ligament detail.

## Neural Activation — How Muscles Contract

**Key structures:**
- **Motor neuron** — nerve cell controlling muscle
- **Motor unit** = one motor neuron + ALL muscle fibres it innervates
- **Neuromuscular junction** — where nerve meets muscle
- **Synapse** — the small gap at the junction

---

## Excitation-Contraction Coupling

The full process from nerve signal to muscle contraction:

```
1. Nerve impulse begins in CNS
2. Travels down motor neuron (facilitated by sodium + potassium electrolytes)
3. Reaches neuromuscular junction
4. Acetylcholine released into synapse (neurotransmitter)
5. Impulse crosses into muscle
6. Sarcoplasmic reticulum releases CALCIUM
7. Calcium triggers myosin heads to bind to actin
8. Myosin heads pull actin toward sarcomere centre (POWER STROKE)
9. Filaments slide past each other → sarcomere shortens → muscle contracts
10. ATP provides energy throughout
11. Nerve stops firing → muscle relaxes, returns to resting length
```

> [!note] Sliding Filament Theory
> Actin and myosin sliding past each other produces contraction. The sarcomere shortens — the filaments themselves do NOT shorten.

---

## Motor Units & the All-or-Nothing Principle

- Motor units either fire COMPLETELY or NOT AT ALL
- Force depends on:
    1. Which units fire (large vs small)
    2. How many fire at once

## Muscle Fibre Types

| Feature | Type I (Slow twitch) | Type II (Fast twitch) |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Slow | Fast |
| Fatigue | Resistant | Fatigues quickly |
| Force | Smaller | Greater |
| Size | Smaller | Larger |
| Motor units | Small | Large |
| Primary use | Endurance, posture, stability | Power, sprinting, jumping |
| Capillaries | Many | Few |
| Mitochondria | Many | Few |
| Myoglobin | High → RED fibres | Low → WHITE fibres |
| Oxidative capacity | High | Low |

> [!note] Memory Trick
> Myoglobin is like hemoglobin (the red pigment in blood) — that's why slow-twitch fibres are red.
> **Red = Slow / White = Fast**

**All muscles contain BOTH types.** Proportions vary by muscle function and genetics. Bigger muscles tend to have more Type II.

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Fibre Types
> Pilates training primarily recruits **Type I (slow-twitch)** fibres through its emphasis on sustained, controlled movement and postural endurance. This is why Pilates builds "functional strength" rather than bulk — it trains the fibres responsible for stability, posture, and fatigue resistance.
> Spring-loaded reformer exercises done explosively can recruit Type II. Programming choice determines which fibres are targeted.

---

## Muscle Roles

| Role | Function | Example (bicep curl) |
|---|---|---|
| **Agonist** (prime mover) | Does the work | Biceps |
| **Antagonist** | Opposes prime mover | Triceps |
| **Synergist** | Assists prime mover | Brachialis |
| **Stabiliser** | Contracts isometrically to prevent unwanted movement | Rotator cuff, TVA |

**Important:** A muscle's role changes based on the movement. The infraspinatus can be:
- Agonist for shoulder external rotation
- Synergist for overhead reach
- Stabiliser for other shoulder motions

---

## Feed Forward Activation

Some muscles (stabilisers especially) contract AUTOMATICALLY in anticipation of movement — before the actual movement begins.

**Example:** Core engages milliseconds before arm movement, protecting the spine preemptively.

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Powerhouse Activation
> The Pilates **powerhouse** relies on feed-forward activation: TVA (transversus abdominis), multifidus, pelvic floor, and diaphragm engage reflexively before limb movement in trained individuals. This is the neurological basis of "engaging your centre" before moving.
> 
> Key stabilisers in Pilates:
> - **TVA** — wraps the trunk like a corset, creates intra-abdominal pressure
> - **Multifidus** — deep spinal stabiliser, vertebra-by-vertebra control
> - **Pelvic floor** — co-activates with TVA for lumbo-pelvic stability
> - **Diaphragm** — closes the "core canister" from above
>
> These are distinct from the global movers (rectus abdominis, obliques) that beginners over-recruit.

## Ageing & Muscle

| Age | What happens |
|---|---|
| 20–30 | Peak mass, power, strength |
| 30–50 | Minimal changes |
| 50+ | Lose 1–2% muscle mass per year |

**Best defence:** Resistance training combats decline for aging adults.
