# Bioenergetics & Energy Systems

---

## Core Concept

> [!note] Definition
> **Bioenergetics** = study of how chemical energy (food) is converted into mechanical energy (movement/work).
>
> Chemical bonds broken → energy released → muscle contractions.

**ATP (adenosine triphosphate)** = the body's universal energy currency.

**The problem:** Cells store ATP in limited quantities. The body must CONTINUOUSLY produce more via three bioenergetic pathways.

---

## The Bioenergetic Continuum

![[../assets/energy-sys-usage.jpg]]

**Analogy:** Three gears of a car
- **1st gear (ATP-PCr)** — explosive burst off the line
- **2nd gear (Glycolysis)** — moderate speed with noise
- **3rd gear (Oxidative)** — cruise control for long haul

> [!note] Key Principle
> All three are ALWAYS running — one just dominates based on intensity and duration.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│        ENERGY SYSTEM CONTINUUM                               │
│                                                              │
│  INTENSITY ─────────────────────────────► DURATION           │
│                                                              │
│  ATP-PCr     ──►  Glycolysis    ──►  Oxidative               │
│  (0-10 sec)      (30-60 sec)         (2+ min)                │
│  ANAEROBIC       ANAEROBIC           AEROBIC                 │
│  90-100% int.    80-90% int.         Low-moderate            │
│                                                              │
│  Fuel:           Fuel:                Fuel:                  │
│  Phosphocreatine Glucose              Carbs, fats, protein   │
│                                                              │
│  ~2 ATP:         2 ATP/glucose        36-38 ATP/glucose      │
│                                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## System 1: ATP-PCr (Phosphagen System)

- Also called the **phosphagen system**
- **ATP + PCr (phosphocreatine)** both stored in muscle cells
- **Anaerobic** — no oxygen needed
- Provides IMMEDIATE energy

**Key facts:**
- Energy for first ~10 seconds of ALL activity
- Activated at onset of any activity (fastest production)
- Used for high-intensity, short-duration bouts
- Think: 90–100% intensity

**Examples:** Sprinting, heavy weightlifting, vertical jumps

**Why it's limited:** Stored PCr runs out quickly. If activity continues, must reduce intensity and transition to glycolysis.

**Everyday examples:** Sprinting to catch a bus, lifting a heavy child, jumping over a puddle, quick dash to answer a phone.

---

## System 2: Glycolysis

- **Anaerobic** — no oxygen
- Breaks down GLUCOSE (sugar/carbs) to produce ATP
- Each glucose molecule → **2 ATP** (through anaerobic glycolysis)

**Two sub-types:**

| Type | Oxygen present? | Duration | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fast glycolysis** | No (anaerobic) | Up to 1-2 min | Lactic acid build-up |
| **Slow glycolysis** | Yes (aerobic) | Longer | No lactic acid (pyruvate → oxidative) |

### Lactic Acid

- By-product of anaerobic glycolysis (pyruvate if can't be utilized fast enough → lactic acid)
- Build-up increases muscle cell acidity
- Interferes with muscle contractions → **performance decreases**

**Duration:** Approximately 30–60 seconds
**Intensity level:** ~80–90%

**Examples:** Typical 8–12 rep resistance training set, 400m run, moderate-to-high intensity circuits.

**Everyday examples:** Climbing stairs quickly, shovelling snow for a few minutes, carrying a load up several flights, quick game of tag.

---

## System 3: Oxidative System

- **Aerobic** — requires oxygen
- Relies primarily on carbohydrates and fats (can use protein)
- Oxygen supplied through respiration — takes time to ramp up

**Process:**
- Similar to anaerobic glycolysis BUT with oxygen
- Pyruvate doesn't convert to lactic acid — becomes substrate for ATP production
- Uses **Krebs cycle + electron transport chain** in mitochondria

**ATP yield:**
- 36 ATP per glucose molecule (up to 38 ATP depending on substrates/pathways)
- **WAY more efficient than glycolysis**

**Duration:** Predominant in activities >2 minutes. Involvement increases over 30 seconds.

**Fuel at low intensity** = primarily fat.

**Examples:** Long jog, bike ride, walking, daily activities (working, standing, sitting).
**Primary energy system for majority of daily life.**

**Everyday examples:** 30-min brisk walk, gardening for an hour, long bike ride, cleaning the house vigorously for an extended period.

---

## Key Principle: Intensity × Duration

**Inverse relationship:**
- Intensity ↑ = Duration ↓
- Intensity ↓ = Duration ↑

Think of it as a trade-off:
- Explosive jump = very short
- 400m run = moderate duration
- Long hike = much longer but easier

## Programming by Energy System

| Client goal | Target system | Programming approach |
|---|---|---|
| Power/explosive strength | ATP-PCr | Short max effort, LONG rest (2–5 min) to replenish PCr |
| Muscular endurance/hypertrophy | Glycolysis | 8–12 reps, 30–90 sec rest, circuit training |
| Cardiovascular/fat loss | Oxidative | Long duration, low-moderate intensity |

### Work-to-Rest Ratios

Understanding energy systems = understanding how to structure rest:
- High intensity → needs longer rest
- Moderate intensity → short rest keeps glycolysis firing
- Low intensity → minimal rest for oxidative adaptation

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Energy Systems in Practice
> - **Classical flows** (minimal rest between exercises) → train glycolytic + oxidative systems
> - **Heavy spring work with full recovery** → trains ATP-PCr (explosive spring-loaded footwork, jump board)
> - **Long, sustained holds** (wall sit, isometric work) → oxidative + glycolytic
> - Understanding this explains why programming choice affects which system adapts — and why a 60-min reformer flow produces different adaptations than a high-intensity interval session.
>

## Cardiorespiratory Fitness Metrics

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| **VO2 max** | Ceiling of cardiorespiratory fitness (max O₂ uptake) |
| **Lactic threshold** | Point where lactic acid accumulates faster than body can clear — can be raised with training |
| **Heart rate recovery** | How quickly HR drops after exercise — faster recovery = better fitness |
