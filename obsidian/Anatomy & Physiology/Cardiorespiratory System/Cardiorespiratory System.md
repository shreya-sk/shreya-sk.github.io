# The Cardiorespiratory System

> [!note] Overview
> The **cardiorespiratory system** = cardiovascular + respiratory systems working together to:
> - Deliver O₂ and nutrients to tissues
> - Remove CO₂ and waste products
> - Ensure optimal cellular function

---

## Part 1 — Cardiovascular System

### The Heart

- Muscular pump, involuntary cardiac muscle
- Located obliquely in thoracic cavity, slightly left of sternum
- Size of adult fist, ~300g (10 oz)
- Similar structure to skeletal muscle — has actin/myosin myofibrils that cross-bridge

### Four Chambers, Two Pumps

![[../assets/heart.jpg]]

```
         LUNGS
          ▲ ▼
    ┌──────────────┐
    │  L.A │ R.A   │  ← ATRIA (smaller, reservoirs, on top)
    │──────┼───────│
    │  L.V │ R.V   │  ← VENTRICLES (larger, pumps, on bottom)
    └──────────────┘
       ▼      ▲
     BODY   BODY
    (oxy)  (deoxy)
```

| Chamber | Role |
|---|---|
| **Right atrium** | Receives deoxygenated blood FROM body (via vena cavae) |
| **Right ventricle** | Pumps deoxygenated blood TO lungs (thin walls, low pressure — short distance) |
| **Left atrium** | Receives oxygenated blood FROM lungs |
| **Left ventricle** | Pumps oxygenated blood TO entire body (THICK walls, high pressure — long distance) |

> [!note] Key Point
> The left ventricle is the primary site for cardiac adaptations from training (it works hardest).

---

### Complete Blood Flow Path

```
BODY (deoxygenated)
  ↓
Superior Vena Cava (upper body) + Inferior Vena Cava (lower body)
  ↓
RIGHT ATRIUM
  ↓ (tricuspid valve — 3 flaps)
RIGHT VENTRICLE
  ↓ (pulmonary artery)
LUNGS ← PULMONARY CIRCULATION
  ↓ (drops CO₂, picks up O₂)
  ↓ (pulmonary veins)
LEFT ATRIUM
  ↓ (mitral valve)
LEFT VENTRICLE
  ↓ (aorta)
BODY (oxygenated) → SYSTEMIC CIRCULATION
```

### One-Way Valves

Each chamber separated by one-way valves to prevent backflow:
- **Tricuspid** — between right atrium and right ventricle
- **Mitral** — between left atrium and left ventricle

---

### Heart's Electrical Conduction System

```
SA Node (right atrium) — the "PACEMAKER"
  ↓ (internodal pathways)
AV Node (between atria and ventricles)
  ↓ (delays impulse so ventricles fill with blood)
Purkinje fibres → myocardial cells contract
  ↓
Normal sinus rhythm
```

> [!note] Memory Tricks
> - **SA node = Start of heartbeat** (sinoatrial pacemaker)
> - **AV node = Available delay** (lets ventricles fill before firing)

---

### Key Heart Measurements

| Term | Definition | Normal |
|---|---|---|
| **Heart rate (HR)** | Beats per minute | 60–100 bpm at rest |
| **Bradycardia** | Low HR | Below 60 bpm |
| **Tachycardia** | High HR | Above 100 bpm |
| **Stroke volume (SV)** | Blood pumped per beat | ~75–80 ml |
| **End-diastolic volume** | Blood filling ventricle BEFORE contraction | — |
| **End-systolic volume** | Blood remaining AFTER contraction | — |
| **SV calculation** | End-diastolic minus end-systolic | — |
| **Cardiac output (Q)** | HR × SV | ~5 L/min at rest |

**Trained athletes** often have lower resting HR — the heart pumps more efficiently.

---

### Blood — Three Mechanisms

Blood = ~8% of body weight, ~5 litres in average adult.

**Components:**
- **Plasma** — watery liquid with nutrients (glucose, lipids, proteins, hormones, clotting agents)
- **Red blood cells** — carry oxygen from lungs throughout body
- **White blood cells** — fight infection
- **Platelets** — clotting + healing (contain growth factors)

**Three support mechanisms:**

| Mechanism | Functions |
|---|---|
| **Transport** | O₂, nutrients, waste, hormones, heat |
| **Regulation** | Body temperature, pH (acid balance — via plasma), water content |
| **Protection** | Clotting, immune cells (fight disease) |

---

### Blood Vessels

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│   Heart → Aorta → Arteries → Arterioles  │
│                                      ↓   │
│          CAPILLARIES (exchange site)     │
│                                      ↓   │
│   Heart ← Veins ← Venules ← Capillaries  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Vessel | Function |
|---|---|
| **Aorta** | Largest artery — carries blood from heart |
| **Arteries** | Carry blood AWAY from heart |
| **Arterioles** | Smaller branches |
| **Capillaries** | Smallest vessels — exchange O₂, CO₂, nutrients, waste |
| **Venules** | Collect blood from capillaries |
| **Veins** | Carry blood BACK to heart (have one-way valves) |

**Major arteries (branches of aorta):**
- **Carotid** — brain
- **Subclavian** — arms
- **Mesenteric** — digestive system
- **Renal** — kidneys
- **Iliac** — pelvic/reproductive organs

**Capillary formation:**
- **Vasculogenesis** — formation of NEW capillaries
- **Angiogenesis** — new capillaries from EXISTING vessels
- Growth factors from platelets aid both

---

### Blood Pressure

- Measurement of blood circulating against artery walls
- Based on: cardiac output + peripheral resistance

| Pressure | When it occurs | Normal |
|---|---|---|
| **Systolic** | Heart contracts | <120 mmHg |
| **Diastolic** | Heart relaxes (filling with blood) | <80 mmHg |

**Hypertension** = chronically elevated BP. Multiple stages.

## Part 2 — Respiratory System

> [!note] Overview
> Also called the **pulmonary system**. Includes airways, lungs, and respiratory muscles.
>
> **Primary role:** Bring O₂ in, transfer to bloodstream, remove CO₂.
>
> **Bonus function:** Filters 7,000–9,000 litres of air daily — removes foreign particles and germs.

---

### Breathing (Ventilation)

**Two phases:**

| Phase | Mechanism | Normal resting state |
|---|---|---|
| **Inspiration** (inhalation) | Active — muscles contract, thoracic cavity expands, intrapulmonary pressure drops below atmospheric, air drawn in | Uses diaphragm + external intercostals |
| **Expiration** (exhalation) | Passive at rest — inspiratory muscles relax. Active in heavy breathing — expiratory muscles compress cavity | — |

### Respiratory Muscles

**Primary (normal breathing):**
- Diaphragm
- External intercostals (between ribs)

**Secondary (heavy breathing):**
- Scalenes (neck)
- Sternocleidomastoid (neck)
- Pectoralis minor
- Abdominal muscles (active expiration)

> [!note] Pilates Reference — Lateral Thoracic Breathing
> The **Pilates breath** is lateral thoracic breathing — the ribcage expands side-to-side and back during inhalation rather than the belly rising. This engages the intercostals maximally, allows the TVA to stay engaged, and facilitates thoracic mobility.
>
> In classical Pilates: **inhale to prepare, exhale to exert effort** (exhalation engages the deep abdominals via the connection between the diaphragm and TVA/pelvic floor).
>
> Visible contraction of secondary respiratory muscles (neck muscles, pec minor) during rest = a red flag indicating dysfunctional breathing patterns. See [[#Abnormal Breathing Patterns|Abnormal Breathing Patterns]].

### Respiratory Rates

| State | Rate |
|---|---|
| Normal rest | 12–16 breaths/min |
| **Tachypnea** | >24 breaths/min (too fast) |
| **Bradypnea** | <8 breaths/min (too slow) |

---

### Respiratory Passageways

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  CONDUCTING AIRWAYS                                │
│  (no gas exchange — just channel/prepare air)      │
│                                                    │
│  Nose/mouth → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea →         │
│  Bronchi → Bronchioles                             │
│                                                    │
│  Purpose: Filter, humidify, warm/cool air          │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  RESPIRATORY AIRWAYS                               │
│  (gas exchange happens here)                       │
│                                                    │
│  At end of bronchioles → ALVEOLI (alveolar sacs)   │
│                                                    │
│  Purpose: Gas exchange via DIFFUSION               │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

### Gas Exchange — The Full Process

```
1. Air inhaled through nose/mouth
2. Travels through trachea, bronchi, bronchioles
3. Reaches alveolar sacs
4. Simultaneously: deoxygenated blood pumped from right ventricle to lungs
5. Blood enters pulmonary capillaries surrounding alveoli
6. Oxygen DIFFUSES from alveoli across capillary membranes into blood
7. Oxygenated blood returns to left atrium via pulmonary veins
8. Pumped to body from left ventricle
9. Cells use O₂, produce CO₂
10. CO₂ carried back to lungs, diffuses into alveoli
11. Exhaled out
```

> [!note] Beautiful Exchange
> As O₂ comes in, CO₂ goes out — they trade places in tissues, blood, and lungs.

---

### Respiratory Pump & Venous Return

- During inspiration, intrathoracic pressure DROPS
- This drops pressure in right atrium
- Helps circulate blood BACK to heart
- **This is why proper breathing improves circulation**

---

### Oxygen Uptake

The body's use of oxygen. Depends on:
1. Respiratory system's ability to COLLECT oxygen
2. Cardiovascular system's ability to ABSORB and TRANSPORT
3. Tissues/cells' ability to USE oxygen

> [!note] Fat Loss & CO₂
> **Critical fact for weight loss clients:** Fat cannot be burned without oxygen. As a carbon-based life form, the only way mass can be physically removed (aside from surgery) is through CO₂ exhaled with every breath.

---

### Abnormal Breathing Patterns — Warning Signs

**Symptoms of rapid/shallow breathing:**
- Dizziness
- Hyperventilation
- Visible contraction of secondary respiratory muscles (neck)
- Altered CO₂/O₂ blood content
- Anxiety feelings

**Consequences of improper breathing:**
- Restricted spine and ribcage movement
- Joint stiffness
- Headaches
- Fatigue
- Poor sleep
- Difficulty with exercise
- Poor circulation

> [!note] Scope of Practice
> If a client shows abnormal breathing patterns, refer to medical professional. Fitness professionals CAN teach correct breathing to enhance performance, health, and wellness.

## Exercise Adaptations

| System | Adaptation with training |
|---|---|
| **Cardiovascular** | ↑ Cardiac output (from stroke volume + HR), ↓ resting HR, new capillaries (capillarisation) |
| **Respiratory** | ↑ Respiratory muscle strength, better O₂ delivery, better CO₂ removal |
| **Muscular** | ↑ Size, strength, endurance. Better motor unit synchronisation and rate coding |

> [!note] Early Strength Gains
> Early strength gains are **nervous system adaptations** — not muscle size. See [[../Nervous System/Nervous System#Early Strength Gains Are Neural|Nervous System]].

## Cardiorespiratory Fitness Metrics

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| **VO2 max** | Ceiling of cardiorespiratory fitness (max O₂ uptake) |
| **Lactic threshold** | Point where lactic acid accumulates faster than body can clear — can be raised with training |
| **Heart rate recovery** | How quickly HR drops after exercise — faster recovery = better fitness |
