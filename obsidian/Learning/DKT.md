# The complete NSW Driver Knowledge Test study guide

**Everything you need to pass the NSW DKT is in this guide.** The Driver Knowledge Test is your first step toward a NSW driver licence, and with a pass mark of 41 out of 45 questions, you need thorough preparation — particularly for the Road Safety section, where only one wrong answer is allowed out of 30 questions. This guide covers every testable topic based on the NSW Road Users' Handbook and Transport for NSW official rules current to 2025–2026. Study it carefully, memorise the key numbers, and you will walk into your test with confidence.

---

## 1. How the DKT works: format, fees, and booking

The DKT consists of **45 multiple-choice questions**, each with three answer options, drawn from a bank of over 550 questions. It is split into two sections that you must pass independently:

|Section|Questions|Pass requirement|
|---|---|---|
|Part 1: General Knowledge|15|At least **12 correct**|
|Part 2: Road Safety|30|At least **29 correct** (only 1 wrong allowed)|

**Two ways to take the test.** The online DKT (via service.nsw.gov.au) is a 4–6 hour interactive course-and-test that costs **$57 for unlimited attempts** — you simply wait 12 hours after a failed attempt before retrying. You can enrol from age 15 years and 11 months and complete it from home. The in-person DKT at a Service NSW centre costs **$57 per attempt**, requires booking, and is available from age 16. There is no formal time limit for either version. The online version is available in English, Simplified Chinese, Arabic, Spanish, and Vietnamese. The in-person test supports additional languages including Croatian, Greek, Korean, Serbian, and Turkish, with interpreter services for other languages.

After passing the DKT, visit a Service NSW centre with proof of identity to apply for your learner licence (**$31 fee**). You do not need to book for this visit.

## 2. The graduated licensing system: learner, P1, P2, and full licence

### Learner licence (yellow L plates)

You can obtain a learner licence from **age 16** after passing the DKT. If you are under 25, you must complete **120 hours of supervised driving** (including at least **20 hours at night**) recorded in either a paper logbook or the official Roundtrip digital app — the only accepted digital logbook since June 2024. Each hour with a licensed NSW driving instructor counts as three logbook hours (up to 10 instructor hours = 30 logbook hours), and completing the Safer Drivers Course adds 20 hours of credit, potentially reducing actual driving time to around 80 hours. Learners aged 25 and over have no logbook requirement.

Your supervisor must hold a **full (unrestricted) Australian driver licence** and sit in the front passenger seat at all times. You must display **yellow L plates** on the front and back exterior of the vehicle. Key restrictions include a maximum speed of **90 km/h** regardless of posted limits, **zero BAC**, an absolute prohibition on mobile phone use (including hands-free and GPS), no towing, and a demerit point threshold of just **4 points**. A single speeding offence carries a minimum of 4 demerit points for learners, triggering automatic suspension. The learner licence is valid for 5 years, and you must hold it for at least **12 months** before progressing to P1 (if under 25).

### Provisional P1 licence (red P plates)

To obtain P1, you must be at least **17 years old**, pass the Hazard Perception Test ($57), and pass a practical driving test ($70). P1 drivers display **red P plates** and are limited to **90 km/h**. The demerit point threshold remains at **4 points**, and all the zero-BAC and total phone-ban rules from the learner licence continue.

P1 drivers under 25 face a **peer passenger restriction**: between 11 pm and 5 am, you cannot carry more than **one passenger under 21** (immediate family members are exempt). All P1 and P2 drivers are **banned from high-performance vehicles** — those with a power-to-tare-mass ratio exceeding 130 kW per tonne. You must hold P1 for at least **12 months** before moving to P2.

### Provisional P2 licence (green P plates)

Moving from P1 to P2 requires no additional test — just apply at a Service NSW centre ($112 for 3 years). P2 drivers display **green P plates** and enjoy a higher speed limit of **100 km/h** and a more generous demerit threshold of **7 points**. The zero-BAC rule and total mobile phone ban still apply. The high-performance vehicle ban continues. You must hold P2 for at least **24 months** before applying for a full licence, and no further test is required for that progression.

### Quick reference: licence conditions

|Condition|Learner|P1|P2|Full|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Max speed|90 km/h|90 km/h|100 km/h|Posted limit|
|BAC limit|0.00|0.00|0.00|Under 0.05|
|Mobile phone|Banned entirely|Banned entirely|Banned entirely|Hands-free/cradle only|
|Demerit threshold|4 points|4 points|7 points|13 points|
|Plates|Yellow L|Red P|Green P|None|
|Minimum holding period|12 months (under 25)|12 months|24 months|—|

## 3. Speed limits you must know by heart

**Default speed limits apply when no speed sign is posted.** In a built-up area (street lights spaced no more than 100 metres apart with buildings beside the road), the default is **50 km/h**. Outside built-up areas, the default is **100 km/h**. The maximum posted speed limit in NSW is **110 km/h**.

**School zones drop to 40 km/h** between **8:00–9:30 am and 2:30–4:00 pm** on all notified school days, including pupil-free days within the school calendar — but not weekends, public holidays, or gazetted school holidays. School zones are marked by signs, flashing lights, and dragon's teeth road markings. These are among the most heavily tested topics on the DKT.

**Shared zones have a 10 km/h limit** where pedestrians and vehicles share the road space. When passing a **stationary emergency vehicle or tow truck with flashing lights**, you must slow to **40 km/h** if the speed limit is 80 km/h or below; on roads above 80 km/h, slow to a safe speed and move over if possible. This rule does not apply if the vehicle is on the other side of a divided road with a median strip.

**Roadwork zones** require you to obey the speed shown on roadwork signs (commonly 40, 60, or 80 km/h). When a **school bus displays flashing amber lights** and a "40 when lights flash" sign, you must not pass at more than **40 km/h** in any direction.

## 4. Give way rules, intersections, and right of way

These rules are the backbone of the DKT. The fundamental principle is: **if you are turning across another vehicle's path, you must give way.**

**At uncontrolled intersections** (no signs, lights, or roundabout), a vehicle turning right must give way to oncoming vehicles going straight or turning left, and to any vehicle approaching from the right. A vehicle going straight must give way to any vehicle approaching from the right. At **T-intersections**, vehicles on the terminating road (the road that ends) must give way to all traffic on the continuing road.

**At stop signs**, you must come to a complete stop before the stop line, then give way to all vehicles in, entering, or approaching the intersection. At **give way signs**, slow down and prepare to stop, giving way to all traffic with the right of way. When **turning at any intersection**, you must give way to pedestrians crossing the road you are turning into — even without a marked crossing. This is a frequently tested rule.

**At traffic lights**, red means stop, green means proceed if safe. A **yellow (amber) light** means you must stop unless you are so close that sudden braking would be dangerous — it never means "speed up." A **green arrow** gives you a protected turn. A **flashing yellow arrow** means you may turn but must give way to pedestrians. When traffic lights are not working, treat the intersection as an **all-way stop**. U-turns at traffic lights are **prohibited unless** a "U-turn permitted" sign is displayed.

**Slip lanes** (separate turning lanes marked by a traffic island) require you to give way to vehicles on the road you are entering, vehicles turning right into that road, and pedestrians crossing.

## 5. Roundabout rules explained clearly

**The key rule: give way to all vehicles already in the roundabout.** The common myth that you only give way to vehicles on your right is incorrect — you must give way to any vehicle already circulating, regardless of where it entered.

**Signalling at roundabouts** is critical DKT content. When turning left, signal left on approach and keep signalling. When going straight, do not signal on approach but signal left as you exit (if practical). When turning right or making a U-turn, signal right on approach, continue signalling right through the roundabout, then signal left as you exit.

**Lane selection in multi-lane roundabouts**: use the left lane for turning left, either lane for going straight, and the right lane for turning right or making a U-turn — unless road markings or arrows direct you otherwise. Always follow painted arrows on the road, as they override the general rules.

## 6. Overtaking, lane changing, and keeping left

**Overtaking is only permitted when safe** — you need clear visibility, sufficient space, and must stay within the speed limit. You must not overtake across unbroken centre lines, near crests or curves with limited visibility, where a vehicle has stopped at a pedestrian crossing, or where the road narrows. You may cross a broken centre line to overtake, and you may cross even double unbroken lines specifically to pass a cyclist while maintaining the minimum safe distance.

**The minimum passing distance for cyclists** is **1 metre** when the speed limit is 60 km/h or below, and **1.5 metres** when above 60 km/h. To maintain this distance, you are permitted to cross centre lines if you have a clear view ahead. Breach of this rule carries a $410 fine and 2 demerit points.

**Overtaking on the left** is only allowed when the vehicle ahead is turning right, is stopped, or you are on a multi-lane road. When a truck displays a "Do Not Overtake Turning Vehicle" sign, it may use more than one lane to turn — stay behind it.

**Lane changing** requires indicating, checking mirrors and blind spots (head check), and giving way to vehicles already in the lane you are entering. When lanes merge without markings (zipper merge), give way to the vehicle that is ahead of you. When moving from a stationary position at the kerb, signal for at least **5 seconds**.

**The keep left rule** applies on multi-lane roads where the speed limit exceeds **80 km/h** or a "Keep left unless overtaking" sign is displayed. You must not drive in the right lane unless overtaking, turning right, avoiding an obstruction, or the left lanes are congested.

## 7. Traffic signs: regulatory, warning, and information

**Regulatory signs must be obeyed.** They typically feature a white background with a red circle or border. Speed limit signs show the number in a red circle. Stop signs are red octagons. Give way signs are inverted white triangles with red borders. No-entry signs show a white bar in a red circle. These signs carry legal force — disobeying them means fines and demerit points.

**Warning signs alert you to hazards ahead** and are typically yellow diamonds with black symbols. Common examples include curve warnings, intersection ahead, pedestrian crossing ahead, school zone, slippery road, road narrows, and railway crossing ahead. Advisory speed signs (yellow background) show recommended speeds for hazards like curves — they are not legally enforceable speed limits but indicate safe speeds in good conditions.

**Information signs** are generally rectangular. Direction signs on freeways and highways are green with white text. Service signs (fuel, food, hospital) are blue with white symbols. Tourist signs are brown with white text. Variable message signs on motorways provide real-time traffic information.

## 8. Road markings and lines: what you can and cannot cross

Understanding road markings is essential for both the DKT and real driving. The rules are straightforward once you learn the pattern:

**A single broken (dashed) centre line** is the least restrictive — you may cross it to overtake, turn, or make a U-turn where permitted. **A single unbroken (continuous) centre line** is more restrictive — you must not cross it to overtake or U-turn, but you may cross it to enter or leave the road (e.g., turning into a driveway). **Double unbroken lines** are the most restrictive — you must not cross them except to enter or leave the road by the shortest route, or to maintain the safe passing distance when overtaking a cyclist.

When double lines have **one broken and one unbroken line**, the rule depends on which line is closest to you. If the broken line is on your side, you may cross. If the unbroken line is on your side, you must not cross to overtake or U-turn.

**Painted islands** (striped sections surrounded by lines) generally must not be driven on. However, you may drive on a painted island surrounded by single lines for up to 50 metres to enter or leave the road. **Keep Clear markings** mean you must never stop in that area. **Edge lines** mark the road boundary. A **continuous yellow kerb line** means no stopping, while a **broken yellow kerb line** indicates a clearway.

## 9. Parking rules and the numbers you must memorise

The DKT tests specific parking distances frequently. **No Stopping** means you cannot stop at all (except emergencies). **No Parking** means you can stop for up to **2 minutes** to pick up or drop off passengers or goods, but you must stay within 3 metres of your vehicle.

### Critical parking distance rules

|Location|Minimum distance|
|---|---|
|Signalised intersection|**20 metres**|
|Unsignalised intersection|**10 metres**|
|Bus stop|**20m before, 10m after**|
|Pedestrian/children's crossing|**20m before, 10m after**|
|Railway level crossing|**20 metres** either side|
|Fire hydrant|**1 metre**|
|Post box|**3 metres**|
|Double dividing lines|**3 metres**|
|Another parked vehicle (front/rear)|**1 metre**|

**Clearways** operate during times shown on the sign — you must not stop during those hours, and vehicles may be towed. **Bus zones** are for public buses only. **Loading zones** allow goods vehicles to park for up to 30 minutes. **Double parking** (stopping beside another parked vehicle in the roadway) is always prohibited.

**When parking on a hill**, turn your front wheels towards the kerb whether facing uphill or downhill — this ensures the vehicle rolls into the kerb rather than into traffic if the brakes fail. Apply the park brake, and leave a manual vehicle in first gear (uphill) or reverse (downhill).

## 10. Alcohol, drugs, and the zero-tolerance rules

**Learner, P1, and P2 drivers must maintain zero BAC at all times.** Full licence holders must stay under **0.05 BAC**. At 0.05, your crash risk approximately doubles. Only time reduces BAC — coffee, showers, and exercise have no effect. Every police car in NSW is a mobile Random Breath Test unit.

Drink driving offences are categorised by severity: **novice range** (any detectable amount for L/P drivers), **low range** (0.05–0.079), **mid range** (0.08–0.149), and **high range** (0.150+). Penalties escalate from fines and licence suspension at the low end to imprisonment and multi-year disqualification at the high end. Mid-range and high-range offenders face mandatory participation in the **Alcohol Interlock Program**, which requires a breath-testing device fitted to the vehicle that prevents it from starting if alcohol is detected.

**Mobile Drug Testing** checks for four illegal drugs via saliva: **THC (cannabis), methamphetamine, MDMA (ecstasy), and cocaine.** NSW operates a zero-tolerance policy — any detectable amount is an offence, regardless of impairment level. Refusing a test is also an offence. Driving while affected by prescription or over-the-counter medication can result in a DUI charge even if the drugs are legally prescribed.

## 11. Demerit points: how quickly you can lose your licence

Demerit points accumulate on your record when you commit offences. Once you reach your threshold within a 3-year rolling period, your licence is suspended. The thresholds are **4 points** for learners and P1 drivers, **7 points** for P2 drivers, and **13 points** for full licence holders. Full licence holders can elect a 12-month good behaviour period instead of suspension (limited to 2 additional points), but this option is **not available** to learner or provisional drivers.

The most important demerit values to know are **5 points** for illegal mobile phone use, **3 points** for not wearing a seatbelt, **3 points** for running a red light, and **1 to 6 points** for speeding depending on how far over the limit. A single mobile phone offence (5 points) exceeds the entire threshold for learner and P1 drivers, causing **automatic licence loss**.

**Double demerit periods** apply during public holiday periods (Christmas/New Year, Easter, Anzac Day, Queen's Birthday) and double the demerit points for speeding, seatbelt, helmet, and mobile phone offences. During these periods, a phone offence would attract **10 demerit points**.

## 12. Seatbelts, child restraints, and mobile phones

**Every occupant must wear a properly adjusted seatbelt.** The driver is legally responsible for ensuring all passengers under 16 are restrained. Passengers aged 16 and over can be individually fined.

**Child restraint rules by age** are strictly defined. Children from birth to 6 months must be in a rear-facing child seat and cannot sit in the front. From 6 months to under 4 years, a rear-facing or forward-facing child restraint with an inbuilt harness is required, and front seats are prohibited. From 4 to under 7 years, a forward-facing restraint or booster seat is required, and the front seat is only permitted if all rear seats are occupied by children under 7. Children 7 and over must use a seatbelt, with booster seats recommended until the child reaches **145 cm** in height.

**Mobile phone rules are strict for all licence classes.** Learner, P1, and P2 drivers face a **total ban** — no phone use whatsoever, including hands-free, Bluetooth, GPS, or music functions, even when stopped at traffic lights. Full licence holders may use phones hands-free or in a commercially manufactured cradle for calls, audio, and GPS, but must never hold the phone or use it for texting, social media, or video calls. NSW deploys AI-powered mobile phone detection cameras that operate day and night, with fines of **$423** (or $562 in school zones) plus **5 demerit points**.

## 13. Sharing the road safely with vulnerable users

**Pedestrians have significant protections.** You must give way to pedestrians on any marked crossing — at zebra crossings, slow down and stop if a pedestrian is on or approaching the crossing. At **children's crossings** (identified by red-orange flags reading "CHILDREN CROSSING"), you must stop and remain stopped until **all pedestrians have completely left the crossing**. When turning at any intersection, give way to pedestrians crossing the road you are turning into, even without a marked crossing.

**Cyclists are legal vehicles** with the same road rights as cars. Respect the minimum passing distances of 1 metre (≤60 km/h zones) and 1.5 metres (>60 km/h zones). Cyclists may ride two abreast, use the full lane, and turn right from the left lane. Always check mirrors before opening your car door — "dooring" a cyclist is a serious offence.

**Heavy vehicles have massive blind spots.** If you cannot see the truck driver's mirrors, the driver cannot see you. Never pull alongside a truck's left side when it is turning left. Trucks displaying "Do Not Overtake Turning Vehicle" signs may legally occupy multiple lanes when turning. Trucks also take **up to 40% longer to stop** than cars — never cut in front of one.

**When an emergency vehicle approaches with sirens or flashing lights**, move left, slow down, and stop or pull over as soon as safely possible. For stationary emergency vehicles and tow trucks with flashing lights, slow to **40 km/h** in zones of 80 km/h or below.

**Give way to buses** in built-up areas when you are in the left lane, the bus is signalling right, and it displays a "Give Way to Buses" sign. This rule applies on roads with speed limits of 70 km/h or less.

## 14. Driving in challenging conditions

**At night**, headlights must be on between sunset and sunrise or whenever you cannot see a person in dark clothing at 100 metres. Use high beam for better visibility but **dip to low beam within 200 metres** of an oncoming vehicle or when following within 200 metres of another vehicle. If dazzled by oncoming headlights, look to the left edge of the road, slow down, and do not look directly at the lights. About one-third of all crashes occur at night.

**In wet weather**, stopping distances increase significantly. Increase your following distance to at least 4 seconds, use low beam headlights, and brake gently. **Aquaplaning** occurs when water builds up between your tyres and the road surface — if it happens, ease off the accelerator without braking sharply.

**In fog**, use **low beam headlights only** — high beam reflects off fog particles and worsens visibility. Use fog lights if your vehicle has them, slow down significantly, and increase following distance to at least 5 seconds. This is a commonly tested trick question: the correct answer is always low beam, never high beam.

**Fatigue is as dangerous as drink driving.** Being awake for **17 hours** impairs driving ability to the same extent as a BAC of **0.05**. A 4-second microsleep at 100 km/h means travelling over 110 metres blind. The DKT will test whether you know that **sleep is the only effective cure** for fatigue — opening windows, turning up the radio, and drinking coffee do not work. The correct action is always to pull over and take a 20-minute power nap.

**Stopping distances increase dramatically with speed** because braking distance grows with the square of speed — doubling your speed quadruples your braking distance. At 60 km/h on a dry road, total stopping distance (including reaction time) is approximately **45 metres**. At 100 km/h, it stretches to roughly **98 metres**. In wet conditions, add another 20–25% to braking distance. Maintain a minimum **3-second following gap** in good conditions and **4+ seconds** in rain, fog, or poor visibility.

## 15. Railway level crossings demand extra caution

When red lights flash at a railway crossing, **you must stop** — even if no train is visible. If boom gates are down or moving, remain stopped. Never drive onto the tracks unless your vehicle can completely clear them. If lights continue flashing after one train passes, stay stopped — another train may be approaching from the other direction. At crossings without lights or gates (common in rural areas), slow down and look and listen in both directions. You must not park within **20 metres** of a level crossing. If your vehicle stalls on the tracks, get everyone out and move at least 50 metres away.

---

## 16. U-turn rules are simpler than you think

U-turns are **allowed** at intersections without traffic lights where no "No U-turn" sign is posted, and across broken dividing lines where you can see clearly and complete the turn safely. U-turns are **prohibited** at traffic lights (unless a "U-turn permitted" sign is displayed), across unbroken or double unbroken dividing lines, on motorways, and at pedestrian crossings and level crossings. A driver making a U-turn must give way to **all vehicles and pedestrians** — no other road user is required to give way to someone performing a U-turn.

---

## 17. Recent rule changes worth knowing for 2025–2026

Several updates could affect DKT content. The **Roundtrip app** replaced "Licence Ready" as the only accepted digital logbook in June 2024. NSW has expanded **AI-powered detection cameras** for mobile phone and seatbelt offences. **Average speed cameras for light vehicles** are being trialled on regional highways with full enforcement from July 2025. All road penalties increased by 3.2% from 1 July 2025 (DKT and HPT fees are now $57 each). The **Demerit Point Reward Program** — which removes one demerit point for unrestricted licence holders who stay offence-free for 12 months — became permanent in early 2026, though it does not apply to learner or provisional drivers.

## 18. Key numbers at a glance and study strategy

Memorise these numbers — they appear repeatedly in DKT questions:

|Number|What it means|
|---|---|
|**40 km/h**|School zones, speed past emergency vehicles (≤80 zone), speed past school buses with flashing lights, shared zones|
|**50 km/h**|Default urban speed limit (no sign posted)|
|**90 km/h**|Max speed for learner and P1 drivers|
|**100 km/h**|Max speed for P2 drivers; default rural speed limit|
|**0.00 BAC**|Learner, P1, and P2 drivers|
|**0.05 BAC**|Full licence holders|
|**200 metres**|Dip headlights distance|
|**1 metre / 1.5 metres**|Cyclist passing distance (≤60 / >60 km/h)|
|**3 seconds**|Following distance in good conditions|
|**5 seconds**|Signal time before pulling from kerb|
|**20m / 10m**|Parking from signalised intersection / unsignalised intersection|
|**120 hours (20 night)**|Supervised driving for under-25 learners|
|**12/15 + 29/30**|DKT pass marks (General + Road Safety)|

**Your study strategy should prioritise the Road Safety section**, where you can only afford one mistake out of 30 questions. Focus heavily on give way rules, pedestrian crossing rules, emergency vehicle rules, and speed limits. Take free online practice tests at sites like driverknowledgetests.com to familiarise yourself with question formats. Study the official NSW Road Users' Handbook (free PDF download from nsw.gov.au). When in doubt on a DKT question, **choose the safest answer** — the test consistently rewards caution. Read every word carefully, as a single word can change the meaning of a question entirely.

## Conclusion

The DKT rewards thorough preparation, not guesswork. The topics that trip up the roughly 30% of candidates who fail are consistently the same: pedestrian crossing rules, emergency vehicle slow-down requirements, roundabout signalling, U-turn restrictions, and the difference between yellow and red traffic light obligations. The critical insight is that the Road Safety section's near-perfect pass requirement of 29 out of 30 means you cannot afford knowledge gaps in any area. Focus your final revision on the key numbers table above, the give way hierarchy at different intersection types, and the strict zero-tolerance rules for learner and provisional drivers on alcohol and phones. With this guide thoroughly studied, you have covered every topic the DKT will test.
